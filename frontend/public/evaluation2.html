01 · Management

CMS‑EMS

What it was

An internal platform with four moving parts

CMS‑EMS is Shakham's internal platform covering content management, a recruitment model, a bug tracking tool, and an employee/project management system. My job was to understand each module, who was building what, and keep things moving.

What I did

Ran the coordination layer

Ran daily standups, coordinated Aryan, Ghanshyam, Shubham and Prathamesh across the Bug Tracking Tool, Recruitment Model and PMS. Tracked the deployment pipeline, escalated blockers, and kept Ankita informed.

Where I went wrong

Missed a deployment deadline by one day

I wasn't taking updates consistently — I assumed if I told someone, it would get done. When asked for proof, I had nothing written. That's when I learned that's not how it works.

What I changed

Logged everything, verified everything

Started logging every task on Miro. Took updates daily, sometimes more. Kept everything in writing instead of verbal commitments. Delivered the missed item the very next morning and sent written updates before every leave day.

02 · Development

QuestHive

What it is

A gamified family / team task platform

Members get XP, level up, earn coins, compete on leaderboards, and redeem rewards. Built to make accountability — for chores, tasks, and shared responsibilities — actually fun.

What I built — solo

14 modules, 78+ endpoints, full production stack

Full authentication with OTP and hCaptcha, real-time WebSocket group chat, a fairness engine with peer review, XP levelling across 20 levels, and a SuperAdmin analytics console. Spring Boot 3.5 + MongoDB Atlas + Next.js, deployed on Render and Vercel.

Proudest feature

The fairness engine

If three or more group members flag a reward as unfair, the task auto-reopens and everyone is notified. I designed and built this logic entirely from scratch — no reference, no template. It's the feature that made the platform feel fair, not just functional.

03 · Research · Client

SGA — Lyros

What it was

A multi-agent AI reasoning platform — for a real client

Lyros is an AI-powered business intelligence and strategic decision-making platform. In the first two weeks, every term was new — orchestrators, RAG pipelines, vector databases, embeddings. I had to get comfortable, fast.

What I contributed

File architecture + a working webhook prototype

Designed the storage architecture for core strategic documents. Built a working prototype where any file change in Google Drive instantly notifies a server via webhook, end to end in under 5 seconds, no polling. Also researched how agents should be assigned depth levels based on query relevance.

How I learned

Built to understand, not just read

Asked questions in every meeting. Got comfortable with the terminology week by week. By the end, I could explain the architecture in my own words.

What this showed me

How a real industry AI product actually gets built

Not just the code — the decisions, the tradeoffs, the documentation, the architecture thinking behind every choice. Unfamiliar is temporary. Curious is permanent.

What real work actually taught me
01

"Verbal is invisible."

If it's not written down — on Miro, in a message, in a doc — it didn't happen. I learned this the hard way, and it changed how I work permanently.

02

"Ownership is knowing where your boundary is."

Understanding exactly what was mine to own versus mine to track — that clarity changed how I coordinated everything afterward.

03

"Communication is harder than code."

I could build 78 endpoints alone. What I couldn't do at first was make sure the right person knew the right thing at the right time. That's the actual hard problem.
